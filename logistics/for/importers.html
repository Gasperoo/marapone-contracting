// The four things eating your landed cost

WHERE IMPORTERS BLEED

PAIN 01

PI says one thing, CI says another

The proforma invoice has 12 SKUs at one price. The commercial invoice arrives with 11 SKUs, two with different unit prices, and a packing list that doesn't match either. Your customs broker calls. The container is already on the water.

→ FIX: Trade Doc Engine reconciles PI, CI, packing list, and origin certificate against the PO before shipping and flags every mismatch.

PAIN 02

Customs holds with vague reasons

"Documentation discrepancy." That's the message. Now you're emailing your broker, your broker is emailing the agent, the supplier is asleep, and your container is sitting in a bonded warehouse racking storage.

→ FIX: The build pre-empts the most common hold reasons by validating documents against destination customs requirements before goods ship.

PAIN 03

Freight invoices don't match the quote

The forwarder quoted $4,200 for the FCL move. The invoice arrives at $5,180 with seven accessorials you didn't expect — port congestion surcharge, ISPS, doc fee, ENS, BAF revision. Nobody has time to fight every line, so you pay.

→ FIX: Invoice Auditor reads each invoice line against the original quote and contracted tariffs, surfaces every variance, and lets you dispute the ones that aren't yours.

PAIN 04

Supplier reliability is a gut feeling

You think Supplier B is more trouble than Supplier A. You can't quite prove it — you'd have to add up doc errors, ETD slips, quality claims, and freight cost overruns by hand across the year. So you keep ordering from both.

→ FIX: Supplier Intel scores every supplier on doc accuracy, on-time, claim rate, and landed cost variance — so the gut feeling becomes a defensible scorecard.

HOW IT SHOWS UP IN YOUR WEEK
