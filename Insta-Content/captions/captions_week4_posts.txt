Facebook Copy:
One missed specification on a $2M construction project can cost $40,000 to $150,000 in change orders. The range depends on where in the construction timeline the conflict surfaces — pre-construction conflicts are cheap to resolve. Field conflicts are not.

Here's the cost structure of a specification-to-drawing inconsistency that reaches the field:

Pre-construction discovery: RFI, design revision, updated drawing, addendum. Cost: engineering time and coordination. Usually a few thousand dollars at most. Timeline impact: minimal if caught early.

Field discovery: Work was performed to the drawing. Spec requires something different. The subcontractor argues the drawing was the governing document. You argue the spec governs. A change order is negotiated. Depending on scope, materials already installed may need to be removed. Structural or MEP modifications may be required. Schedule impact creates downstream delay claims. On a $2M project, the documented range for this scenario is $40,000–$150,000 in total cost impact. On a $10M project, that range scales accordingly.

The AI system that would have caught the inconsistency in pre-construction review has a one-time cost for most of our clients that falls below the low end of that range.

The math on this investment is straightforward. The first time the system catches a spec conflict that would have become a field change order, the build has paid for itself. Every catch after that is pure margin protection.

The same logic applies to freight: a fuel surcharge calculation error recovered through AI-driven invoice verification pays for a meaningful portion of the system build the first time it's caught at scale. A customs classification error avoided through pre-filing review eliminates duty liability and potential penalty exposure.

These aren't edge cases. They're the regular, predictable costs of working at volume with complex documents.
