WHAT THIS STRATEGY IS

This document is a comprehensive 30-day go-to-market plan for Marapone Contracting
Inc. to establish brand presence on Instagram and Facebook, build a qualified audience
of potential clients in the construction and logistics sectors, and convert at least one
prospect into a paying engagement within 30 days.

The strategy covers organic content, paid advertising, direct outreach, and lead
conversion — each channel working together toward a single defined outcome: one client,
one project, real revenue.

WHO IT IS FOR

The immediate target is the person writing the cheque: general contractors (GCs),
project managers on active commercial projects, freight brokers, 3PL operators,
importers, and B2B operators who manage document-heavy workflows and are currently
losing time or money to manual review processes.

WHY THIS APPROACH

Marapone's strongest asset is its clarity of offer. One-time payment. Full source code.
Private deployment. No subscriptions. This is a positioning that almost no competitor
can match cleanly, and it is the kind of offer that resonates immediately with operators
who have been burned by SaaS subscription costs or cloud data exposure risks.

The approach leverages that positioning as the central message across every channel.
Rather than trying to compete on awareness volume against well-funded SaaS competitors,
this strategy focuses on depth of message and precision of targeting — reaching the
right 1,000 people with a message that lands, rather than the wrong 100,000 people with
a message that doesn't.

The free 24-hour AI assessment is the keystone conversion mechanism. It removes all
financial risk from the first interaction, demonstrates concrete value before asking for
a dollar, and gives Marapone a legitimate reason to contact every prospect who submits
documents.

WHY NOW

AI adoption in construction and logistics is accelerating. Operators who have been
reluctant are beginning to evaluate tools seriously. The SaaS AI market is simultaneously
over-promising and under-delivering, creating meaningful differentiation for a company
that can demonstrate real, specific results quickly. This is the optimal window to
establish positioning before the space becomes crowded with better-funded imitators.

CONSTRUCTION AI MARKET

Pain Points (Decision-Maker Level):
- Blueprint review for complex commercial sets consumes 4–6 hours of senior staff time
  per project, at exactly the moment when that staff is most needed for bid strategy
- RFI backlog is endemic on multi-trade commercial projects; a 48–72 hour response
  cycle is considered normal, despite most answers existing in the original documents
- Change orders caused by pre-construction coordination failures are the single largest
  contributor to margin erosion on fixed-price contracts
- Daily log compilation and progress reporting are time sinks with low direct value
- Manual document review degrades with fatigue, creating inconsistent quality at volume

Budget Availability:
- Commercial GCs operating at 5–15% margin on projects ranging from $500K–$50M have
  meaningful budget authority for tools that demonstrably protect margin
- A $4,900–$9,500 tool purchase is a line item that most project managers or VP-level
  operators can approve without board sign-off
- The ROI case is exceptionally strong: a single caught coordination conflict that would
  have become a $40,000–$300,000 field change order justifies the entire build cost

Decision Makers:
- Owner/operator at firms with 5–50 employees (direct budget authority)
- VP of Construction or Director of Preconstruction at mid-size firms (50–200 employees)
- Senior estimator with PM responsibility (influence, sometimes authority)
- IT/operations manager at firms beginning to evaluate AI formally

LOGISTICS AND FREIGHT AI MARKET

Pain Points:
- Freight invoice discrepancy rates averaging 12–15% across the industry mean most
  brokers and importers are paying overcharges as a cost of doing business
- Manual verification at volume (50–500 invoices/month) is not economically feasible
- Customs entry errors create compliance exposure and retroactive duty liability
- Carrier contract complexity (accessorial tables, fuel surcharge matrices, zone charts)
  makes line-level verification prohibitive without automated tooling
- Trade documentation errors — commercial invoice, packing list, bill of lading
  discrepancies — are common and expensive when they result in holds or re-filings

Budget Availability:
- A regional freight broker or 3PL moving $500K–$5M in annual freight spend is losing
  $60K–$750K annually to undetected overcharges at the 12–15% industry average
- A system recovering even 25% of that exposure pays for itself in weeks
- Small importers ($500K–$2M freight spend) will see this as a cost-reduction tool
  rather than a technology investment — which is a much easier budget conversation

Decision Makers:
- Owner/operator at small-mid freight brokerages and import operations
- VP or Director of Logistics and Operations
- Finance lead at companies with high invoice volume

WHY THE TIMING IS RIGHT

1. SaaS AI Fatigue: Businesses that adopted ChatGPT-based tools in 2023–2024 are
   beginning to see the limits of generic models on their specific documents. Appetite
   for purpose-built, private alternatives is growing.

2. Data Privacy Concerns: Enterprise-level conversations about cloud AI data exposure
   are filtering down to mid-market operators. The question "where does our data go?"
   is now being asked before adoption, not after a breach.

3. Subscription Cost Pressure: In a tighter economic environment, recurring SaaS costs
   are under scrutiny. A one-time ownership model has genuine appeal against a backdrop
   of subscription fatigue.

4. Lack of Competition in Private Deployment: Very few firms offer the specific
   combination of private deployment, full source code handover, industry-specific
   fine-tuning, and boutique service that Marapone provides.

COMPETITOR LANDSCAPE

Tier 1 — General AI Platforms (ChatGPT, Microsoft Copilot, Google Gemini):
- Cheap or free, widely known, but generic; not fine-tuned for construction or logistics
- Cloud-based: data privacy concerns significant for sensitive project documents
- No ownership; subscriptions; no customization for specific workflows
- NOT a direct competitor for qualified buyers — they've already tried these tools

Tier 2 — Construction-Specific SaaS AI (Procore AI, Autodesk AI, PlanGrid AI):
- Deep integration with project management platforms some GCs already use
- Expensive: typically enterprise-tier pricing with per-seat or per-project billing
- Shared cloud infrastructure; no private deployment option
- Positioned as integrated add-ons, not standalone AI systems

Tier 3 — Freight and Logistics SaaS AI (Flexport AI, Transplace AI):
- Large enterprise focus; pricing and onboarding unsuitable for small-mid operators
- SaaS model with ongoing subscription dependency
- Generic freight intelligence, not contract-specific audit tools

Tier 4 — Independent AI Consultancies:
- Build custom models but typically require ongoing consulting retainer
- No clean handover; creates dependency on the consultancy
- Higher cost, longer timeline, unclear ownership model

MARAPONE'S UNIQUE POSITIONING AND COMPETITIVE ADVANTAGES

1. Ownership Model: The only sustainable differentiator at this price point. No
   competitor in the addressable mid-market offers full source code handover plus
   fine-tuned model weights at $4,900–$15,000 all-in.

2. No Recurring Fees: This is a direct financial argument, not just a positioning
   claim. The math against SaaS alternatives over 3–5 years is definitively in
   Marapone's favour.

3. Industry Specificity: Fine-tuned models trained on construction and logistics
   documents outperform generic AI on domain-specific tasks. This is demonstrable.

4. The Free Assessment: A risk-free first step that creates a concrete, tangible
   deliverable before any money changes hands. Most competitors require demo calls,
   trial signups, or upfront commitments. Marapone delivers real findings on real
   documents with zero financial risk.

5. Radical Transparency: No fake case studies, no enterprise client logos never
   worked with, honest about team size and capacity. In an industry full of
   over-promise, this is a trust-building advantage.

6. Speed: 2–4 week builds vs. 3–6 month enterprise implementation timelines.

7. Canadian Roots, B2B Trust: Small team, direct communication, no layers of sales
   process between prospect and the people actually doing the work.

THE CORE NARRATIVE: OWNERSHIP VS. RENTAL

The central positioning of Marapone is not about AI. It is about ownership.

Every SaaS AI company on the market is renting operators their intelligence back to
them on a monthly basis. Marapone builds the intelligence and hands it over permanently.
This is a fundamentally different value proposition — and it has clear, specific financial
implications that make it easy to reason about.

The narrative is: "Every month you pay for AI software, you're building someone else's
revenue. We build something that belongs to you."

This narrative:
- Is instantly understandable to any business operator
- Creates a competitive moat (few competitors can credibly match it)
- Generates a clear financial comparison (SaaS cost over 5 years vs. one-time build)
- Appeals to operators' sense of autonomy and risk management
- Works across all three personas without modification

EXECUTION: The ownership narrative should appear in some form in every piece of
content — post captions, stories, ad copy, DM outreach, email subject lines. It is the
brand's North Star and should be consistent enough that the audience can articulate it
on their own after 2 weeks of exposure.

TRUST-BUILDING STRATEGY FOR A NEW BRAND

A new brand selling a high-value service ($1,500–$15,000) into a conservative,
relationship-driven industry (construction) must earn trust before it can earn revenue.
Trust is built through:

1. Specificity over generality: Specific numbers, specific outcomes, specific document
   types. "Catches freight invoice discrepancies" is vague. "$18,400 recovered in month
   one from 47 flagged invoices" is credible.

2. Transparency about limitations: Explicitly acknowledge what the system does not do.
   ("AI flags, humans decide." / "We don't do residential.") Counterintuitively, these
   admissions build more trust than avoiding them.

3. The free assessment as proof of quality: Every prospect who submits documents gets
   a real deliverable. If the findings are useful, the work speaks. If they are not,
   acknowledge it honestly. The assessment is not a sales tool; it is a demonstration.

4. Consistent, professional content quality: The visual brand — dark backgrounds, orange
   accents, clean typography — is already strong. Maintaining that quality standard
   across every post signals that this is a serious operation.

5. Radical transparency about team and process: The "who we are" narrative (small team,
   Canada and Italy, 10+ years industry experience, no fake enterprise logos) is a
   differentiator, not a liability. Lead with it.

THE FREE ASSESSMENT AS CONVERSION MECHANISM

The free 24-hour AI assessment is the most important marketing asset in the strategy.
It should be the CTA on virtually every piece of content.

Why it works:
- Zero financial risk eliminates the primary objection
- The 24-hour turnaround creates urgency and demonstrates capability
- Real documents, real findings — this is a demonstration of product quality, not a
  demo in a controlled environment
- Creates a legitimate, welcome reason to follow up with the prospect
- Starts the sales relationship with Marapone in a position of having already
  provided value

How to communicate it consistently:
- Lead with what the prospect will receive: specific findings on their documents
- Clarify the non-salesy nature: "No commitment required. No sales call at the end
  unless you want one."
- Reinforce the 24-hour timeline: this signals speed and responsiveness
- Vary the framing each week without changing the offer itself

CONTENT PILLARS AND RATIONALE

Four content pillars, each serving a specific audience function:

PILLAR 1 — THE PROBLEM (25% of content)
Articulate the pain points that Marapone solves. Speak directly to the day-to-day
frustrations of GCs, freight brokers, and B2B operators. No product mention needed —
just accurate pain articulation that makes the right prospect feel seen.
Rationale: People follow accounts that understand their world. This builds audience
before it builds pipeline.

PILLAR 2 — THE EDUCATION (30% of content)
Explain how private AI works, why ownership matters, what the build process looks like,
how fine-tuning differs from generic AI. Make the audience smarter about the category
without being condescending.
Rationale: Educated prospects close faster and with fewer objections. Education content
also performs well with shares and saves — signals that feed the algorithm.

PILLAR 3 — THE PROOF (25% of content)
Case studies, testimonials, specific outcomes, before/after comparisons. Numbers where
possible. Real client quotes. The case for Marapone made by results rather than claims.
Rationale: This is the content that converts skeptics and gives warm leads the final
push they need. Also the content most likely to be shared with a decision-maker by
someone who saw it.

PILLAR 4 — THE OFFER (20% of content)
Direct calls to action for the free assessment. Urgency framing, offer reinforcement,
objection handling. The explicit ask.
Rationale: Content that never asks for anything never gets anything. The offer pillar
ensures every follower who is ready to take a step knows exactly what that step is.

WHEN TO START PAID ADS

Launch on Day 8 (beginning of Week 2). Rationale: The first 7 days of organic content
establish a baseline of 5–10 posts on the profile. A cold prospect who sees an ad and
visits the profile needs to see enough content to establish credibility and intent.
Running ads to an empty profile wastes budget and undermines trust.

Do NOT wait for Week 3. Every day of delay on paid ads is a day the pixel is not
accumulating data, which limits the effectiveness of retargeting.

CAMPAIGN STRUCTURE

Campaign 1 — AWARENESS (Cold Audiences)
- Ad Set 1: Construction audience (Ad 01 copy)
- Ad Set 2: Logistics audience (Ad 02 copy)
- Objective: Traffic (link clicks / landing page views)
- Budget: $15–$20 CAD/day per ad set
- Run period: Days 8–30

Campaign 2 — RETARGETING (Warm Audiences)
- Ad Set 1: Website visitors (past 30 days, did not submit form)
- Ad Set 2: Post engagers and profile visitors (past 30 days)
- Objective: Conversions (form submissions) or Traffic
- Budget: $10–$15 CAD/day (increase to $15–$20 in Week 4)
- Run period: Days 8–30 (audience size will be small early; grows over time)

Campaign 3 — CONVERSION (Hot Audiences)
- Ad Set 1: Visited /contact or /pricing in past 14 days, did not submit
- Ad Set 2: 2+ page views in past 14 days (high-intent signal)
- Objective: Conversions (form submission)
- Budget: $8–$12 CAD/day (increase to $12–$18 in Week 4)
- Run period: Days 8–30 (audience populates as campaign runs)

Days 1–7: $0 (organic only)
Days 8–21 (14 days):
  - Campaign 1: $35–$40/day × 14 days = $490–$560

META PIXEL SETUP (REQUIRED BEFORE RUNNING ADS)

Install the Meta Pixel on marapone.com before launching any paid ads. Set up the
following custom conversion events:
1. Contact form view (/contact page view) — tracking intent
2. Form submission (Standard Event: Lead) — tracking actual assessment requests
3. Pricing page view (/pricing page view) — tracking commercial intent
4. Any "thank you" page after submission — confirming conversion

Without these events, retargeting and conversion campaigns will not have the data
they need to optimize, and you will not be able to accurately track cost per lead.

TRACKING ROAS

ROAS (Return on Ad Spend) should be tracked at the campaign level:
- Primary metric: Cost per form submission (lead)
- Secondary metric: Cost per link click
- Tertiary metric: Reach and frequency (avoid frequency above 3 for cold audiences)

A single $4,900 client acquired through a $1,500 total ad spend is a 3.3x ROAS —
exceptional for B2B. A $9,500 client on the same spend is 6.3x. Track this.

Google Analytics or Meta's own reporting will show cost per form submission if the
pixel is installed and conversion events are configured correctly.

THE FUNNEL STAGES

Stage 1 — STRANGER
Definition: Target-profile individual who has not encountered Marapone.
Action: Organic content distribution, paid awareness ads, hashtag reach, engagement
on industry accounts. They discover Marapone through content, ad, or comment.
Conversion trigger: First exposure to the "you're renting your AI" message.
Goal: Follow or profile visit.

Stage 2 — FOLLOWER
Definition: Has followed @maraponehq or visited the website.
Action: Content quality must validate their decision to follow. Week 1–2 content
establishes the narrative and begins building trust.
Conversion trigger: A post that directly articulates their specific pain point.
Goal: Save a post, share content, leave a comment.

Stage 3 — ENGAGER
Definition: Has liked multiple posts, left a comment, saved content, or clicked a link.
Action: This is the DM outreach trigger. The warm prospect outreach script applies here.
Retargeting ads also activate for this audience.
Conversion trigger: Retargeting ad or direct DM with the free assessment offer.
Goal: Visit marapone.com/contact.

Stage 4 — LEAD
Definition: Has visited the contact or pricing page.
Action: Conversion retargeting ad (Ad 04) activates. Direct follow-up if they came
via a tracked link and you have their contact information.
Conversion trigger: Ad 04 copy addressing their specific hesitation directly.
Goal: Submit the free assessment form.

Stage 5 — ASSESSMENT
Definition: Has submitted documents and is awaiting findings.
Action: Deliver findings within 24 hours. Quality of findings is the entire sales pitch.
Findings should be: specific, referenced to their actual documents, non-generic,
honest about what would and wouldn't be addressed in a build.
Conversion trigger: Specific, valuable findings create the natural "what would a full
system look like?" question.
Goal: Follow-up call booked.

Stage 6 — CLIENT
Definition: Has agreed to move forward with a paid engagement.
Action: Scope confirmation, pricing agreement, contract and payment.
Conversion trigger: Clear proposal with defined scope, timeline, and outcome.
Goal: Signed agreement and deposit received.

POST-ASSESSMENT FOLLOW-UP SEQUENCE

Within 24 hours of submission:
Send findings. Email (or via the method they provided). Be specific. Reference their
actual documents. Do not use boilerplate language. State clearly what a full system
would catch and what tier would be appropriate.

Day 2 (if no reply to findings):
"Hi [name], just wanted to confirm you received the assessment findings — let me know
if any questions came up reviewing them."

Day 5 (if still no reply):
"Hi [name], I know these decisions take time. Happy to answer any questions about what
a build would look like for your operation — no pressure on timing."

Day 10:
"[Name] — leaving this thread open in case the assessment findings are useful in the
future. Happy to pick up whenever it makes sense for you."

Do not follow up beyond Day 10 without a specific new reason (new case study,
relevant news, their situation has changed).

ASSESSMENT TO SALE CONVERSION TACTICS

1. Make the findings specific and impressive: Generic AI observations won't convert.
   Specific findings referenced to their actual documents will.

2. End the findings report with a clear next step: "Based on what we found, I'd suggest
   the [Pilot / Full Build] tier. Here's what that would cover and what it would cost."

3. Offer a brief follow-up call: "Happy to walk through the findings on a 20-minute
   call if that's useful." No pressure — just an offer. Most interested prospects
   will take it.

4. During the follow-up call: Ask questions before pitching. Understand their current
   workflow, their current costs, their primary constraint. Build the proposal around
   their specific answer, not a generic scope.

5. The proposal: Keep it short (one page). Define scope, timeline, price, and what they
   receive at handover. Include the guarantee. Ask for a decision on the call or within
   48 hours.

Batch by week, not by the full 30 days. Review what performed well in Week 1 before
finalizing Week 2 captions. The SVG post files are already produced — caption
adaptation and scheduling is the primary production task.

Recommended batch schedule:
- End of Week 1 (Day 7): Review Week 1 performance; finalize and schedule Week 2 posts
- End of Week 2 (Day 14): Review; finalize and schedule Week 3 posts
- End of Week 3 (Day 21): Review; finalize and schedule Week 4 posts (adjust CTAs
  based on how many assessment submissions have come in)

SECTION 12 — RISK FACTORS AND CONTINGENCY PLANS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

RISK 1: LOW ORGANIC ENGAGEMENT IN WEEK 1–2

Trigger: Under 50 followers by Day 7 and/or under 10 meaningful comments in first 2 weeks.

Response:
1. Audit the content quality and posting times. If using the existing SVG files and
   captions, quality is not the likely issue — distribution is.
2. Double the engagement effort on target accounts. The algorithm rewards accounts
   that engage first.
3. Increase the paid awareness ad budget by $10–$15/day earlier (Day 5 instead of Day 8).
4. Share content to LinkedIn and ask your existing network to engage (even 20 genuine
   reactions from real people can signal the algorithm positively).
5. Evaluate hashtag effectiveness: if no hashtag reach is showing in Insights, the
   hashtag sets may be too large (too competitive) — shift to more niche sets.

RISK 2: NO ASSESSMENT SUBMISSIONS BY DAY 14

Trigger: Zero submissions through the contact form by the end of Week 2.

Response:
1. Check the contact page: Is the form functional? Is the link in bio pointing to the
   correct URL? Check this first — form submission failures are a common technical issue.
2. Immediately activate direct outreach. Do not wait for content to generate inbound.
   Identify 10–15 target companies (GCs or freight brokers in your market) and send
   the cold email template from Section 9. This should generate at least 1–2 responses.
3. Lower the friction on the assessment. If the form asks for too much information,
   simplify it — the only thing needed to start an assessment is a document and an
   email address.
4. Shift Week 3 content earlier: Move proof/case study posts to Days 11–14 rather
   than waiting for Week 3. The social proof content tends to trigger assessment
   submissions more directly than education content.
5. Offer the assessment via DM, not just via the website form. Some prospects will
   find it easier to share documents in a conversation.

RISK 3: ASSESSMENTS DELIVERED BUT NOT CONVERTING TO SALES

Trigger: 3+ assessments delivered with findings but no paid engagement proposed or
agreed to by Day 21.

Response:
1. Audit the findings quality. Are they specific enough? Are they referencing the
   prospect's actual documents with specific issues found? If findings are generic,
   they won't create urgency.
2. Review the follow-up sequence. Are follow-ups being sent within 24 hours? Is the
   next step being proposed explicitly in the findings document?
3. Consider a temporary introductory offer for first clients: "For our first two builds
   this quarter, we're offering the Pilot tier at $3,900 CAD (regular $4,900). This is
   to allow us to gather specific performance data to share publicly." This reduces
   price resistance without permanent discounting.
4. Shift the CTA in follow-ups from "book a call" to "let me answer any questions by
   email first." Some prospects are not ready for a call but will engage in text.
5. Ask for honest feedback: "What's the main hesitation?" Most prospects who don't
   convert will tell you if asked directly and respectfully.

RISK 4: PAID ADS NOT GENERATING QUALIFIED TRAFFIC

Trigger: Ads running but cost per link click above $4–5 CAD, or clicks not converting
to contact form views.

Response:
1. Test the ad creative — switch to the video asset (blueprint-analysis-demo.mp4)
   instead of static graphics. Video typically has lower CPCs.
2. Narrow the audience targeting: reduce geographic scope, tighten job title targeting.
3. Test the landing page: if clicks are not becoming page views or form visits, the
   landing page (marapone.com/contact) may have a friction issue. Simplify the above-
   the-fold message or add a direct testimonial near the form.
4. Pause the underperforming ad variant and allocate the budget to the variant showing
   better click-through rates. Do not continue running both if one is clearly weaker
   after 7 days of data.

SECTION 13 — FIRST CLIENT ACQUISITION STRATEGY
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

THE FASTEST PATH TO A BOOKED CALL

The fastest route to a first client is not waiting for inbound — it is identifying
5–10 specific target companies and reaching out directly in parallel with the
organic and paid campaign.

How to find direct outreach targets:
- LinkedIn: Search "General Contractor Toronto" (or your primary market). Filter by
  company size (11–200 employees), sort by activity. Look for decision-makers with
  recent posts about project activity, document challenges, or technology.
- Google: "[City] general contractor commercial" — visit company websites, identify
  the owner or VP of operations, find their email (often on the contact page or
  discoverable via LinkedIn).
- Freight/Logistics: Search "freight broker [city]" or "3PL [city]" on LinkedIn
  and Google. Industry associations (CIFFA in Canada, TIA in the US) have member
  directories.
- Trade shows and associations: Canadian Construction Association, OGCA, BCCA
  have member directories. The Canadian International Freight Forwarders Association
  (CIFFA) is another.
- Instagram and Facebook: Construction and logistics accounts posting about their
  operations are your warmest outreach targets. They're already on your platform.

The direct outreach sequence:
Day 1: Follow the target on Instagram. Like or comment genuinely on 1–2 posts.
Day 3: Send connection request on LinkedIn with the outreach note from Section 9.
Day 5: Cold email (template from Section 9).
Day 8: LinkedIn follow-up message.

This is the most compressed path to a first engagement. Do not wait for inbound
to generate the first client.

PRICING STRATEGY FOR THE FIRST ENGAGEMENT

Do not discount the first client as a standard offer. The pricing reflects real value
and should not be undercut preemptively. If a prospect objects to price, use the
ROI argument (a single caught coordination conflict or a month of recovered freight
overcharges justifies the cost) before considering any adjustment.

If price remains the sticking point after the ROI conversation:
- Offer the Starter tier ($1,500 CAD) as a paid proof-of-concept: one detailed
  document review, written findings, video walkthrough. This positions the lower
  tier as a pilot, not a discount.
- Offer a structured payment: 50% on project start, 50% on handover. This reduces
  upfront risk without reducing the total price.
- Do not offer more than 15% off any tier, and frame it explicitly as a first-client
  introduction offer, not a permanent price point.

CLOSING STRATEGY

1. Before the close: Confirm that the decision-maker is on the call. If you've been
   talking to someone who needs to get approval, ask directly: "Is there anyone else
   involved in this decision we should loop in for the next conversation?"

2. On the close call: Summarize the assessment findings, confirm their specific problem,
   state the recommended tier and its scope, quote the price, and ask for the decision.
   "Based on what we found in your documents and what we've discussed, I'd recommend
   the [Pilot / Full Build]. That's [price]. Does that work for you?"

3. If they need time: Set a specific follow-up time. "Happy to give you a few days to
   think on it — should we schedule a 15-minute call Thursday to pick this back up?"
   Vague "I'll get back to you" follow-ups lose deals.

4. When the deal is agreed: Send the scope and invoice within 24 hours. Momentum is
   fragile — delays between verbal agreement and written confirmation lose clients.

HOW TO LEVERAGE THE FIRST CLIENT FOR FUTURE MARKETING

1. Get specific results in writing: After the build, ask the client for specific
   performance data — hours saved per week, overcharges recovered, specific issues
   caught. These numbers become the case study content for Month 2.

2. Get a testimonial on record: Ask for a written quote (or a 2-minute video if they're
   comfortable). The quote in their own words, referencing a specific outcome, is worth
   more than any ad creative you'll ever run.

3. Ask for referrals: The construction and logistics industries are highly networked.
   "Is there anyone in your network — another GC, a freight broker you work with —
   who might benefit from what we built for you?" One satisfied client in the right
   network can generate 2–3 additional leads.

4. Make them a permanent case study: With permission, build a named or anonymized case
   study post for Week 3 of your next 30-day campaign. The more specific, the better.
