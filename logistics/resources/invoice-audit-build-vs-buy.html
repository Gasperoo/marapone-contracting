INVOICE AUDIT AI: BUILD vs BUY.

Most freight brokers leak 3-5% on uncaught carrier-invoice errors. The market for fixing that has matured into three distinct shapes: vendor SaaS, in-house engineering, and custom builds. Each one is a real answer; only one is right for any given shop.

By the Marapone team · Updated 2026

The 3-5% leak, in concrete numbers

Take a brokerage moving $20M of freight a year. Industry studies put uncaught invoice errors at 3-5% of carrier spend — call it a midpoint of 4%. That's $800,000 a year you're paying that you didn't owe. Even if your number is half that, the leak funds an entire FTE.

The errors aren't random. They cluster around fuel surcharge miscalculation, duplicate accessorial charges, missed volume discounts, invalid detention claims, and reweighs that don't match your origin documents. A pattern-detection problem the moment you frame it that way.

Option 1: Vendor SaaS

Multiple vendors offer hosted invoice-audit platforms. The pitch is straightforward: feed them your invoices and contracts, they flag discrepancies, you dispute them.

Where it wins: fast onboarding (days, not weeks), no engineering required, the rules library is mature, and recovery is often share-of-savings so the up-front spend is small.

Where it stalls: your invoices and your shipper contracts leave your network. The vendor's rules library may not match your specific carriers' surcharge logic. You're locked into their dispute workflow and reporting format. Recovery rates that look like 10% in the demo land at 3-4% in practice.

Honest test:

Ask the vendor for the median customer's actual recovery rate over 12 months, not the demo number. The gap between those two is your honest expected value.

Option 2: In-house build

If your engineering team is strong and has bandwidth, building it yourself is feasible. The architecture isn't exotic: an invoice OCR pipeline, a rules engine, a UI for ops to review flags.

Where it wins: the rules can be tuned exactly to your contracts and your dispute style. You own everything. Your team learns the domain deeply.

Where it stalls: realistic calendar is 3-6 months for v1, and it competes with every other priority your team has. ML expertise is a hire if you don't have it. Maintenance becomes a permanent line item — surcharge tables change, carriers change formats, accessorial codes evolve.

The honest cost framing: 1-2 senior engineers for ~4 months at fully loaded comp. Roughly $80-150k in opportunity cost. Plus ongoing maintenance.

Option 3: Custom build (Marapone-style)

The third path: a vendor builds you a private system, hands over the source, and you own it. Same end result as in-house, faster calendar, lower up-front cost.

Where it wins: ~2 weeks to v1 (vs 3-6 months in-house). $9,500 one-time vs $80-150k loaded engineering. Source code shipped so your team can extend later. Your data never leaves your network. No share-of-savings — you keep 100% of recoveries.

Where it stalls: requires a vendor relationship for the build. Less flexibility than building in-house if your needs are highly unusual. Not the right answer for under 5-10 users where SaaS is cheaper than the build cost amortized.

A decision matrix that actually helps

Three questions sort most decisions cleanly:

  1. Is your annual invoice volume above ~5,000? Below that, vendor SaaS pencils out. Above it, the share-of-savings adds up to the cost of a build very quickly.
  2. Are your shipper contracts unusual (custom accessorials, non-standard fuel formulas, FTA-specific terms)? If yes, custom rule fitting matters and a generic vendor's library will miss errors. Custom build wins.
  3. Do you have a senior engineering team with bandwidth? If yes, in-house is feasible. If no, custom build wins on calendar alone.

The recovery-rate question

Vendors sell on recovery. Honest framing: a well-tuned rules engine, regardless of who builds it, recovers 80-90% of the dollar value of errors that exist in your invoices. The variance between "vendor SaaS" and "custom build" on recovery rate is small. The variance is in cost, ownership, and how well the rules match your specific business.

The real recovery-rate question isn't "vendor vs build." It's "are we auditing at all?" Most brokers under 50 employees aren't, and the leak is real.

A worked five-year comparison

$20M annual freight spend, ~10,000 invoices/year, 3.5% uncaught error rate (so $700k annual leak, recovery ceiling).

  • SaaS at 25% share-of-savings: recovers ~$560k/yr, vendor takes $140k/yr. 5-yr cost: $700k. Recovered net: $2.1M.
  • Custom build: $9,500 one-time + ~$10k/yr ops & retainer. Recovers ~$560k/yr. 5-yr cost: $59,500. Recovered net: $2.74M.
  • In-house build: $120k loaded engineering + ~$30k/yr ongoing. Recovers ~$560k/yr. 5-yr cost: $270k. Recovered net: $2.53M.

For this shape of brokerage, custom build wins on net recovery by $640k over five years vs SaaS, and by $200k vs in-house. Your numbers will differ; the structure will not.
